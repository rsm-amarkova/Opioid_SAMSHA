Analysis & Code

Summary of the research question, variables, modelling strategy, and key regression results.

Statistical Story

1. Research Question

We ask whether proximity to SAMHSA-affiliated clinics attenuates the relationship between low income and opioid overdose burden at the county level. Do counties with stronger treatment infrastructure fare better than expected given their socioeconomic disadvantage?

2. Key Variables

  • Outcome: drug overdose deaths in 2023 (drug_od_count_2023).
  • Socioeconomic drivers: median household income, unemployment rate, and a small set of additional demographic controls captured in county_df.
  • Treatment access: total number of SAMHSA-affiliated clinics in each county (SAMSHA_total_count).
  • Opioid environment: opioid dispensing rate per 100 persons.

3. Model Outline

We estimate an ordinary least squares regression in which overdose deaths are regressed on income, unemployment, opioid dispensing, and clinic availability:

drug_od_counti = β₀ + β₁·incomei + β₂·unemploymenti + β₃·dispensingi + β₄·clinicsi + εi.

The Tableau views complement this by showing where low-income, high-burden counties coincide with sparse clinic coverage.

4. Linking back to the visuals

The heat map highlights geographic clusters of high overdose burden and reveals which of those areas currently have SAMHSA clinics. The income view shows how counties move along the income distribution as burden increases. Counties that sit in the “low income, high overdose, few clinics” corner of the joint distribution are the most concerning from an access perspective.

Methods & Data

Overview of how the overdose and clinic datasets were prepared, merged, and translated into the county-level variables used in the analysis.

Data construction and modelling pipeline

Data sources

  • CDC overdose data: county-level counts of opioid-involved overdose deaths and population totals, used to compute overdose rates and total death counts.
  • SAMHSA facility data: geocoded locations of SAMHSA-affiliated treatment facilities, including county and state identifiers.
  • Socioeconomic indicators: median household income, unemployment, and insurance coverage from county-level statistical releases.

Preprocessing

  • Filter all datasets to a common year (2023) and to U.S. counties with valid FIPS codes.
  • Aggregate SAMHSA facilities to the county level to obtain SAMSHA_total_count.
  • Merge overdose, clinic, and socioeconomic data using county FIPS codes.
  • Compute the outcome variables: raw overdose counts and rates per 100,000 residents.

Constructed variables

  • Overdose burden: total overdose deaths in 2023 and overdose rate per 100,000 residents.
  • Socioeconomic indicators: median household income, unemployment rate, and percent uninsured.
  • Treatment access: number of SAMHSA-affiliated clinics per county; in descriptive work we also consider an indicator for having at least one clinic.
  • Income groups: above vs. below the national median income for scatterplots and descriptive comparisons.

Estimation and interpretation

Models are estimated using ordinary least squares. The income and unemployment coefficients capture socioeconomic gradients in overdose burden, while the dispensing rate summarises exposure to prescribed opioids. The coefficient on SAMSHA_total_count is small and only marginally significant, suggesting that simple cross-sectional counts of clinics are not enough to demonstrate a strong protective effect once other factors are taken into account.

Policy framing

Rather than concluding that clinics are ineffective, we interpret the results as capturing a snapshot of a dynamic process: places with severe overdose problems may be precisely the communities that decide to invest in more clinics. The dashboards and model together highlight where those investments appear most needed—counties that are both economically disadvantaged and heavily burdened by overdose deaths.

Limitations & Future Directions

Reflections on what we would change with more time and data, and how the story around clinics and overdoses should be interpreted.

What we would change with a longer horizon

1. Cross-section vs. trajectory

Our analysis is based on a single cross-section of counties. This lets us describe where overdoses and clinics are today, but it does not reveal how communities arrived at those configurations. In reality, the story likely runs as follows:

cities experience rising overdoses → local leaders notice the crisis → clinics are installed or expanded in response.

In that world, a county could have both high overdose counts and many clinics, not because clinics are ineffective, but because they were deployed to confront an already severe problem. Our static regression cannot disentangle this feedback loop.

2. Need for longitudinal monitoring

Ideally, we would follow counties over time and examine whether overdose trends improve after clinics are introduced or expanded. With panel data, we could:

  • Track overdose rates before and after clinic openings.
  • Compare counties that gained clinics to similar counties that did not.
  • Use designs such as difference-in-differences to estimate a more credible causal impact.

That kind of longitudinal monitoring would align better with the policy question our story is hinting at: do clinics help bend the overdose curve once they are in place?

3. What our results should — and should not — be used for

The current project is best viewed as a descriptive mapping exercise rather than definitive evidence on clinic effectiveness. We can say:

  • Where overdose burden is highest.
  • Where treatment infrastructure is thin relative to need.
  • How burden correlates with income, unemployment, and dispensing rates.

We cannot say that clinics “do not work” based on these results. Instead, a more cautious interpretation is that communities facing high overdose burden are installing clinics in response, and our cross-sectional snapshot is catching them mid-course.

4. Future extensions

With more time, we would like to:

  • Incorporate multiple years of overdose and clinic data to study trajectories.
  • Distinguish between different types of facilities (inpatient, outpatient, MAT, etc.).
  • Overlay additional structural factors such as transportation access and rurality.

These extensions would sharpen the story from “where are clinics and overdoses now?” to “how are communities using treatment infrastructure to respond to an evolving crisis?”
